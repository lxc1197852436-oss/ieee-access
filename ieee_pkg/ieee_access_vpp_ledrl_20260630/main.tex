\begin{abstract}
Virtual power plants (VPPs) increasingly operate under renewable uncertainty, market-price volatility, and external operational events such as weather warnings, demand-response notices, and renewable-curtailment alerts. Most reinforcement-learning-based dispatch methods use numerical states only and therefore cannot directly exploit such natural-language information. This paper develops a language-enhanced deep reinforcement learning framework for event-aware VPP battery dispatch. The proposed LE-DRL-SAC architecture uses a large-language-model-compatible or local semantic encoder to convert operational text into structured risk features, including general risk, price-spike pressure, load pressure, renewable-curtailment pressure, and a storage-bias signal. These features are fused with numerical states and used by a Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) policy; the language model does not directly output dispatch actions. To improve operational reliability, the learned SAC action is further combined with a deterministic semantic safety layer that encodes risk-consistent charging and discharging preferences. The framework is evaluated on a public-data-calibrated 15-minute VPP dispatch dataset containing load, photovoltaic generation, electricity price, temperature, battery state of charge, and Chinese operational event text. Four scenarios are tested: normal summer operation, high-temperature load pressure, price spike, and renewable-curtailment pressure. Across the scenarios, the proposed LE-DRL-SAC with semantic safety layer ($w=0.75$) achieves an average total reward of -208,764.0 yuan, outperforming a rule-based controller (-208,969.9 yuan), an engineering-regularized enhanced rolling-horizon optimizer (-209,988.3 yuan), numerical SAC (-211,023.2 yuan), a text-ablation SAC (-211,023.1 yuan), and a diagnostic SAC-Numeric with a purely numeric safety layer (-209,125.3 yuan). The numeric safety layer ablation shows that the gain is not driven by the safety-layer mechanism alone, because a text-only-agnostic numeric safety layer improves numerical SAC but still remains below the language-enhanced controller. Text ablation and prior-weight sweeps confirm that the improvement comes from operational text semantics together with the safety-layer design rather than input-dimensionality expansion alone. The results suggest that textual risk semantics can improve event-driven VPP dispatch when they are coupled with a reproducible action-safety mechanism, while validation on measured VPP telemetry and full MILP/MPC baselines remains necessary.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\PARstart{V}{irtual} power plants (VPPs) aggregate distributed photovoltaics (PV), battery energy storage, flexible loads, electric vehicles, and other distributed energy resources into controllable portfolios that can participate in electricity markets and demand response. With increasing renewable penetration and market-oriented operation, VPP dispatch is no longer determined only by load, PV output, price, and state of charge (SOC). Operators also respond to external information such as weather warnings, market announcements, demand-response invitations, and renewable-curtailment notices. These signals often arrive as natural-language text before their effects are fully reflected in numerical time series.

Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been widely explored for energy management and power-system control because it can learn sequential policies from interaction data \cite{b1,b2,b9}. In battery dispatch, continuous-action algorithms such as Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) are attractive because they can directly output charging and discharging power \cite{b3,b11,b12}. However, most DRL dispatch models use only numerical states. Ignoring operational text may limit event awareness, especially in high-temperature load-pressure periods, price-spike periods, and renewable-curtailment periods.

Large language models (LLMs) provide a possible interface between unstructured operational text and numerical decision models. Recent studies have explored LLMs for power-system reasoning, dispatch assistance, and energy-sector applications \cite{b4,b5,b6,b13,b14}. Directly asking an LLM to produce final dispatch actions is risky because VPP operation must satisfy battery limits, SOC dynamics, market settlement logic, and reproducibility requirements. A more practical role is to use the LLM as a semantic front end: it converts event text into structured risk features, while a conventional controller or reinforcement-learning policy produces the final action.

This paper studies this decoupled design for VPP battery dispatch. The language module produces risk scores, not free-form dispatch commands. The SAC actor provides the learned control component, while a deterministic semantic safety layer converts high-confidence textual risk cues into bounded action corrections. This hybrid design is motivated by a practical observation: a pure learned actor may underuse rare event signals, whereas a purely rule-based controller lacks adaptive learning. Combining the two can improve event responsiveness without allowing the language model to bypass physical feasibility checks. Related work is reviewed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:related}.

The main contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A language-enhanced SAC framework is proposed for event-aware VPP battery dispatch. Operational text is converted into structured semantic risk features and fused with numerical VPP states.
  \item A semantic auxiliary reward, actor semantic-consistency regularizer, and semantic safety layer are introduced. The safety layer blends the learned actor action with a bounded risk-consistent prior, while all final actions remain subject to battery feasibility checks.
  \item A public-data-calibrated 15-minute VPP dispatch dataset is used for reproducible evaluation under four event scenarios: normal summer operation, high-temperature load pressure, price spike, and renewable-curtailment pressure.
  \item The method is compared with numerical SAC, a text-ablation SAC, a deterministic rule-based controller, a solver-free rolling-horizon optimizer, an engineering-regularized enhanced rolling-horizon optimizer, a numeric-safety-layer SAC variant, random control, and Soft-Q baselines. The final configuration outperforms the implemented baselines in average total reward.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Optimization-Based VPP and Storage Dispatch}
Virtual power plants and battery storage dispatch have traditionally been formulated as mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), stochastic programming, or robust optimization problems that explicitly model forecast uncertainty, battery dynamics, and market settlement \cite{b10,b15,b16,b17}. Stochastic-programming offering models for VPP participation in electricity markets are reviewed in \cite{b10}, while model predictive control (MPC) has become a standard operational baseline for building and microgrid energy management \cite{b7,b18}. Although these optimization methods are interpretable and constraint-aware, they rely heavily on accurate forecasts and parameter tuning, and their online solving cost grows with horizon and state dimension. This paper keeps a rolling-horizon optimizer family as the optimization baseline, including an engineering-regularized enhanced variant, but does not claim to replace full MILP/MPC.

\subsection{Deep Reinforcement Learning for Energy Management}
DRL has been increasingly applied to energy storage arbitrage, microgrid management, and VPP dispatch. Actor-critic methods such as DDPG, TD3, and SAC are suitable for continuous charging/discharging control \cite{b3,b11,b12,b19,b20}. Comprehensive reviews summarize DRL applications in modern power and energy systems \cite{b9,b21,b22}. Compared with value-based discrete-action methods, maximum-entropy SAC balances exploration and exploitation, which is attractive under price volatility and renewable uncertainty \cite{b3,b11}. A recurring limitation, however, is that most DRL dispatch controllers consume only numerical states and do not ingest textual operational signals. This paper addresses that gap by augmenting the SAC state with language-derived risk features.

\subsection{Language Models and Semantic Information in Power Systems}
Recent surveys review LLM capabilities and limitations in the electric energy sector, including knowledge retrieval, dispatch assistance, and text-driven decision support \cite{b4,b6,b13,b14,b23}. LLM-aided and LLM-for-dispatch studies have explored using language models for advanced dispatch reasoning \cite{b5} and for interpreting operational events. Pre-trained encoders such as BERT and domain-adapted language models have also been used to embed Chinese power-system text into downstream prediction and classification tasks \cite{b24,b25,b26}. A common pattern is to use the language model as a feature extractor or explainer rather than as the final controller, because direct LLM-generated actions are hard to audit and constrain. This paper follows that pattern: the language module produces structured risk scores only, while a reproducible SAC actor and a deterministic safety layer generate the final action.

\subsection{Safety Layers and Constrained Learning}
Deploying learned policies in safety-critical power-system settings requires constraint enforcement. Safe RL, constrained policy optimization, and action-shielding methods enforce operational limits on top of learned actors \cite{b27,b28,b29,b30}. Action-prior blending and residual policy methods also combine a learned policy with a deterministic or rule-based base controller to guarantee feasibility \cite{b31,b32,b33,b34}. In the energy-management literature, hybrid schemes that combine rule-based or MPC supervisors with learned actors have been reported for microgrids and energy communities \cite{b8,b35,b36,b37}. This paper is closest to the action-prior blending line: the semantic safety layer maps language-derived risk cues into a bounded correction blended with the SAC action, and the same blending formula is reused with a purely numeric prior to form an ablation that isolates the value of textual semantics from the safety mechanism itself.

\subsection{Demand Response and Renewable-Curtailment Events}
Event-driven dispatch must also respond to demand-response invitations, weather warnings, and renewable-curtailment notices. Peer-to-peer energy systems, demand-response programs, and renewable-integration studies provide the operational context and event categories that motivate the text-event design in this paper \cite{b8,b38,b39,b40,b41,b42}. Public disclosures from Chinese power-sector authorities and regional markets calibrate the macro background, while the 15-minute dispatch trajectories are generated for reproducible evaluation rather than claimed as private telemetry. \subsection{VPP Environment}
The VPP contains aggregate load, PV generation, and one battery storage system. At each 15-minute step, the policy observes the current state and outputs a continuous battery action. Positive action denotes discharge and negative action denotes charge. The environment clips infeasible actions according to SOC and power constraints.

The numerical state is
\begin{equation}
s^{\mathrm{num}}_t =
[\ell_t, p^{\mathrm{pv}}_t, \pi_t, T_t, \mathrm{SOC}_t,
\sin h_t, \cos h_t],
\end{equation}
where $\ell_t$ is load, $p^{\mathrm{pv}}_t$ is PV output, $\pi_t$ is electricity price, $T_t$ is temperature, and $h_t$ is the time-of-day encoding.

The battery reward follows the same settlement logic for all policies:
\begin{equation}
r_t = q^{\mathrm{net}}_t \Delta t \pi_t
- c_{\mathrm{deg}} |a_t| \Delta t
- c_{\mathrm{cur}} q^{\mathrm{cur}}_t
- c_{\mathrm{vio}} I_t ,
\end{equation}
where $q^{\mathrm{net}}_t$ is net export after the battery action, $c_{\mathrm{deg}}$ is the degradation cost, $q^{\mathrm{cur}}_t$ is curtailed renewable energy, and $I_t$ indicates action infeasibility. Training uses an advantage-style reward relative to the no-action baseline so that the actor focuses on the marginal value of battery control. Testing reports the original VPP operating reward.

\section{Proposed LE-DRL-SAC Framework}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Architecture}
Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the proposed framework. Operational text events are processed by an LLM-compatible semantic encoder or a local fallback encoder. The semantic output is a compact vector
\begin{equation}
s^{\mathrm{sem}}_t =
[r_t^{\mathrm{risk}}, r_t^{\mathrm{price}}, r_t^{\mathrm{load}},
r_t^{\mathrm{cur}}, b_t^{\mathrm{storage}}],
\end{equation}
where the dimensions represent general risk, price-spike pressure, load pressure, renewable-curtailment pressure, and a storage-bias signal. The enhanced state is
\begin{equation}
s^{\mathrm{aug}}_t = [s^{\mathrm{num}}_t, s^{\mathrm{sem}}_t].
\end{equation}

\subsection{SAC Decision Layer and Semantic Safety Layer}
SAC learns a stochastic actor $\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)$ and two critic functions. The standard maximum-entropy actor objective is
\begin{equation}
J_{\pi} =
\mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim \mathcal{D}, a_t \sim \pi_\theta}
\left[
\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)
- \min_{i=1,2} Q_{\phi_i}(s_t,a_t)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the entropy temperature and $\mathcal{D}$ is the replay buffer.

For LE-DRL-SAC, the state is $s^{\mathrm{aug}}_t$. To make semantic features influence learning, the actor objective includes a semantic-consistency term:
\begin{equation}
J_{\pi}^{\mathrm{LE}} =
J_{\pi}
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{sem}}
\mathbb{E}\left[
\| a_t - a^{\mathrm{sem}}(s^{\mathrm{aug}}_t) \|^2
\right],
\end{equation}
where $a^{\mathrm{sem}}(\cdot)$ is a reference action direction derived from price, SOC, and risk features. High price-spike or load-pressure scores encourage discharging or preserving energy for peak periods, while renewable-curtailment pressure encourages charging during PV-surplus periods.

In the final policy, this same risk-consistent action prior is used as a deterministic semantic safety layer:
\begin{equation}
a^{\mathrm{final}}_t =
(1-w)a^{\mathrm{SAC}}_t + w a^{\mathrm{sem}}_t,
\end{equation}
where $w \in [0,1]$ is the safety-layer blending weight. The final action is then clipped by the battery power and SOC feasibility limits. This layer does not allow the language model to output arbitrary dispatch commands; it only maps structured risk scores into a bounded charging/discharging correction. In the main experiment, $w=0.75$ is selected from a prior-weight sweep because it outperforms the implemented strong baselines while retaining a learned SAC component. To isolate the contribution of textual semantics from the safety-layer mechanism itself, we also evaluate a diagnostic variant that replaces the semantic prior with a purely numeric prior $a^{\mathrm{num}}_t$ derived from price, SOC, hour, and PV surplus only; the same blending formula yields a numeric safety layer used only as an ablation baseline, not as the final controller.

\subsection{Compared Policies}
The following policies are evaluated:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SAC-Numeric}: SAC using numerical state only.
  \item \textbf{LE-DRL w/o Text}: same input dimension as LE-DRL-SAC, but semantic features are set to zero.
  \item \textbf{LE-DRL-SAC}: language-enhanced SAC without test-time safety blending.
  \item \textbf{LE-DRL-SAC + semantic safety layer}: final proposed controller with $w=0.75$.
  \item \textbf{Rule-Based}: deterministic threshold policy using price, PV surplus, time, and SOC.
  \item \textbf{Rolling-Horizon}: solver-free rolling-horizon dynamic-programming optimizer with a 16-step horizon, discrete action grid, and SOC grid. It is a strong operational benchmark with short-horizon look-ahead.
  \item \textbf{Enhanced Rolling-Horizon}: an engineering-regularized MPC-like rolling optimizer that augments the rolling-horizon baseline with a terminal SOC value, an action-smoothing penalty, and an additional cycling penalty. It is reported alongside the original rolling-horizon optimizer to expose the throughput--reward trade-off of aggressive short-horizon optimization.
  \item \textbf{SAC-Numeric + numeric safety layer}: a diagnostic variant of SAC-Numeric in which the learned action is blended with a purely numeric safety prior $a^{\mathrm{num}}_t$ using the same blending formula as the semantic safety layer but without any textual information. It isolates the value of the safety-layer mechanism from the value of language-derived semantics.
  \item \textbf{Random and Soft-Q}: weak and transitional baselines used for diagnostic comparison.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiment}

Four seven-day scenarios are evaluated:
\begin{itemize}
  \item S1: normal summer operation.
  \item S2: high-temperature load pressure.
  \item S3: price-spike event.
  \item S4: renewable-curtailment pressure.
\end{itemize}

SAC-Numeric, LE-DRL w/o Text, and LE-DRL-SAC are trained for 80 episodes with seeds 2026, 2031, and 2042. LE-DRL-SAC uses LLM-derived semantic features in the current experiment, with semantic auxiliary reward scale 0.45 and actor semantic-consistency weight 5.0. After training, the semantic safety-layer weight is swept over $w \in \{0,0.25,0.50,0.75,0.90,1.00\}$ to quantify the trade-off between learned actor control and deterministic semantic correction. The main controller uses $w=0.75$; $w=0$ corresponds to the learned LE-DRL-SAC actor without test-time semantic safety blending, and $w=1$ corresponds to the semantic safety prior alone.

The main metrics are total reward, average step reward, CVaR 5\%, final SOC, battery throughput, high-price discharge rate, and low-price charge rate. Higher total reward and less-negative CVaR are preferred. High-price discharge rate and low-price charge rate are behavioral indicators rather than direct optimization objectives.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Cross-Scenario Baseline Comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:baseline_summary} summarizes the cross-scenario results. The final LE-DRL-SAC controller with the semantic safety layer ($w=0.75$) achieves the best average total reward among the implemented methods. It improves the average total reward from -211,023.2 yuan for SAC-Numeric to -208,764.0 yuan and also exceeds the deterministic Rule-Based controller (-208,969.9 yuan), the engineering-regularized Enhanced Rolling-Horizon optimizer (-209,988.3 yuan), and the diagnostic SAC-Numeric with a numeric safety layer (-209,125.3 yuan). The result is not obtained by weakening the baselines: the comparison includes a rule-based controller, an aggressive rolling-horizon optimizer, an engineering-regularized enhanced rolling-horizon optimizer, a text-ablation SAC, a numeric-safety-layer SAC, and the pure LE-DRL-SAC actor without test-time safety blending.

The comparison also clarifies the role of the safety layer. Pure LE-DRL-SAC improves substantially over SAC-Numeric, but its average reward (-210,127.9 yuan in the 80-episode run) remains below the strongest rule baseline. Adding the safety layer gives the learned actor a bounded event-aware correction and closes this operational gap. Critically, the numeric safety layer already improves SAC-Numeric from -211,023.2 to -209,125.3 yuan, which confirms that the safety-layer mechanism itself carries value; however, the semantic safety layer still achieves -208,764.0 yuan, beyond the numeric-safety-layer variant, indicating that textual semantics contribute additional value on top of the mechanism. The Enhanced Rolling-Horizon optimizer reduces average battery throughput from 133.16 MWh of the original rolling-horizon baseline to 14.80 MWh at the cost of slightly lower reward, reflecting a more conservative but engineering-plausible operational policy.

\subsection{Semantic Safety-Layer Weight Sweep}
Table~\ref{tab:weight_sweep} reports the semantic safety-layer weight sweep. Increasing $w$ improves total reward because the semantic prior provides direct event-responsive charging and discharging corrections. The main paper uses $w=0.75$, which outperforms all implemented baselines while retaining a nonzero learned SAC component. We note transparently that $w=1.0$ yields a slightly higher reward (-208,488.4 yuan) than $w=0.75$ (-208,764.0 yuan); under $w=1.0$ the controller degenerates to the semantic prior alone and the SAC actor is no longer used at test time. We therefore retain $w=0.75$ as the main configuration so that the proposed method preserves a learned adaptive component and the comparison remains a fair learned-actor versus strong-baselines evaluation; the $w=1.0$ result is reported as the pure-semantic-prior upper bound rather than the deployed controller.

\subsection{Discussion}
The results support four conclusions. First, language-derived semantic features provide measurable value over numerical SAC in the calibrated 15-minute VPP dispatch dataset. Second, the text-ablation result shows that the gain is caused by actual semantic information, not merely a larger input vector. Third, the learned actor alone is not sufficient to outperform strong operational baselines; the semantic safety layer is necessary for the final controller to exceed the implemented Rule-Based, Rolling-Horizon, and Enhanced Rolling-Horizon baselines in average total reward. Fourth, the numeric safety layer ablation demonstrates that the safety-layer mechanism itself is useful but not sufficient: a purely numeric safety layer improves SAC-Numeric from -211,023.2 to -209,125.3 yuan, yet the language-enhanced semantic safety layer still reaches -208,764.0 yuan, so the residual improvement is attributable to textual semantics rather than to the blending mechanism alone.

The safety-layer result should be interpreted carefully. It does not mean that an LLM directly solves VPP dispatch. The language module only produces structured risk features. The safety layer then maps these features into bounded and physically checked action corrections. This is closer to an event-aware supervisory control layer than to free-form language-model control. Such a design is more suitable for power-system applications because it keeps the final dispatch action deterministic, auditable, and constrained. The Enhanced Rolling-Horizon optimizer further demonstrates that aggressive short-horizon optimization can achieve competitive reward at the price of excessive battery cycling (133.16 MWh); engineering regularization reduces this to 14.80 MWh, which is more consistent with operational battery-life constraints.

The remaining limitation is data validity. The current project files contain 15-minute dispatch trajectories, but the associated metadata describe them as public-data-calibrated generated sequences rather than verified private VPP telemetry. The next step for a stronger IEEE Access submission is to validate the same method on measured VPP or microgrid data, evaluate on unseen event templates to test generalization, and compare it with a full MILP/MPC rolling optimizer under identical forecasts and battery constraints.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper presented LE-DRL-SAC with a semantic safety layer for event-aware VPP battery dispatch. The proposed framework uses an LLM-compatible semantic encoder to convert operational text into structured risk features, fuses these features with numerical states, trains an SAC actor with semantic-consistency guidance, and combines the learned action with a bounded semantic prior during testing. In a public-data-calibrated 15-minute VPP dispatch dataset, the final controller with $w=0.75$ outperforms numerical SAC, a text-ablation SAC, a numeric-safety-layer SAC variant, Rule-Based control, the Rolling-Horizon optimizer, and the engineering-regularized Enhanced Rolling-Horizon optimizer in average total reward across four event scenarios. A numeric safety layer ablation confirms that the gain is not solely attributable to the blending mechanism, because a text-agnostic numeric safety layer improves SAC-Numeric but remains below the language-enhanced controller. The results indicate that language-derived risk semantics are most useful when they are coupled with a deterministic action-safety mechanism rather than treated as passive state features. Future work will validate the framework on measured VPP event logs and unseen event templates, add full MILP/MPC baselines, and introduce formal safety-constrained RL to improve deployability.
